# Momentum & Nesterov

## The problem SGD faces in narrow valleys

Real loss landscapes often take the form of a **narrow valley** (ravine) — curvature differs dramatically across directions. One direction changes steeply (high curvature), another changes slowly (low curvature).

SGD must use a small enough learning rate to avoid oscillating in the high-curvature direction, but this causes extremely slow progress in the low-curvature direction.

**Condition number** $\kappa = L/\mu$ — ratio of largest to smallest curvature of the loss. When $\kappa$ is large:
- SGD: needs $O(\kappa)$ steps to converge
- Momentum: needs only $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$ steps

For $\kappa = 100$: SGD needs ~100 steps, Momentum needs ~10.

## Heavy Ball momentum (Polyak, 1964)

Add "inertia" — instead of discarding all velocity each step, keep a fraction of the old one:

$$
v_{t+1} = \beta v_t + \nabla\mathcal{L}(\theta_t)
$$

$$
\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta\, v_{t+1}
$$

$\beta \in [0,1)$ is the momentum coefficient — typically $\beta = 0.9$.

**Unrolling** reveals that $v_t$ is an exponentially weighted sum of past gradients:

$$
v_t = \sum_{k=0}^{t} \beta^{t-k}\,\nabla\mathcal{L}(\theta_k)
$$

In oscillating directions, gradients keep flipping sign → cancel out in $v$. In consistent directions, gradients point the same way → accumulate → larger effective step.

**Effective gradient amplification**: near convergence, $v \to \frac{1}{1-\beta}\nabla\mathcal{L}(\theta)$. With $\beta = 0.9$: gradient is amplified $10\times$ — the learning rate must be scaled down accordingly compared to plain SGD.

<Note type="warning">
Heavy Ball has no general convergence guarantee for non-quadratic convex functions and can actually diverge. The $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$ proof applies only to quadratics with $\beta$ tuned optimally given prior knowledge of $\kappa$.
</Note>

## Nesterov Accelerated Gradient (Nesterov, 1983)

Heavy Ball computes the gradient at the *current position* $\theta_t$. Nesterov computes it at the *position momentum will carry us to* — the "look-ahead":

$$
\theta_{\text{look}} = \theta_t - \eta\beta v_t
$$

$$
v_{t+1} = \beta v_t + \nabla\mathcal{L}(\theta_{\text{look}})
$$

$$
\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta\, v_{t+1}
$$

**Intuition**: if we're moving fast toward the minimum, the gradient at $\theta_{\text{look}}$ will already be smaller (or correcting direction) — allowing us to "brake" earlier and avoid overshooting. Heavy Ball only discovers the gradient at the old position, so it learns too late that it's about to overshoot.

<Note type="theorem">
Nesterov achieves $O(1/t^2)$ convergence for smooth convex functions, vs $O(1/t)$ for GD — and this rate is **optimal** among all first-order methods. In practice, the main benefit is smoother convergence and less overshoot compared to Heavy Ball.
</Note>

## Convergence summary

| | GD | Heavy Ball | Nesterov |
|---|---|---|---|
| Quadratic | $O(\kappa)$ | $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$ | $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$ |
| Smooth convex | $O(1/t)$ | no guarantee | $O(1/t^2)$ |
| Deep learning practice | — | good | better |

Both Heavy Ball and Nesterov achieve $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$ on quadratics, but Heavy Ball requires knowing $\kappa$ to set $\beta$ optimally, while Nesterov is more adaptive.

## In practice

```python
# PyTorch
optimizer = torch.optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.01, momentum=0.9)                # Heavy Ball
optimizer = torch.optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.01, momentum=0.9, nesterov=True) # NAG
```

- $\beta = 0.9$: widely used default. Can go up to $0.95$–$0.99$ for smooth problems.
- When increasing $\beta$: reduce $\eta$ accordingly, since the effective gradient scale grows as $1/(1-\beta)$.
- Momentum is less common in modern LLM training — Adam already incorporates momentum ($\beta_1 = 0.9$).

## Connection to modern optimizers

- **Adam**: uses EMA of gradients (1st-order momentum, $\beta_1$) plus EMA of $g^2$ (2nd-order momentum, $\beta_2$) for per-dimension scaling
- **Muon** (2024): Nesterov momentum + orthogonalization of the gradient matrix — designed for weight matrices in LLMs
