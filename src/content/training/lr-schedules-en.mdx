# LR Schedules

## Why not use a fixed learning rate?

A fixed learning rate creates an irresolvable trade-off:

- **Large LR**: good exploration, escapes local minima, but oscillates around the minimum at the end of training instead of converging
- **Small LR**: converges well, but makes extremely slow progress early on

The natural solution: **large lr early, small lr late** — this is the core idea behind every LR schedule.

## Warmup — why is it needed?

Warmup is a phase where the lr increases from near zero to lr_max over the first few hundred to few thousand steps.

**Reason with Adam**: $v_t$ (second moment) is initialized to zero and needs time to build a reliable estimate. If you use lr_max immediately while $\hat{v}_t$ is still small (not yet accumulated), the actual step size is very large → gradient explosion or training instability.

**Reason with large models**: weight initialization creates an unstable gradient landscape. Warmup lets the model "orient itself" before receiving a large lr.

<Note type="intuition">
Warmup is like warming up a car before driving at full speed — without it, you risk "breaking" the optimizer state from the very start.
</Note>

Typical warmup duration: **1–10% of total steps** (a few hundred steps for fine-tuning, a few thousand for LLM pre-training).

## Common schedules

<LRViz lang="en" />

### Step decay

$$
\eta_t = \eta_0 \cdot \gamma^{\lfloor t / s \rfloor}
$$

Reduces lr by a fixed factor (typically $\gamma = 0.1$) at pre-defined milestones. Simple — was the standard in vision (used in the ResNet paper). Drawback: milestones must be chosen manually, and lr changes abruptly.

### Cosine annealing

$$
\eta_t = \eta_{\min} + \frac{1}{2}(\eta_{\max} - \eta_{\min})\left(1 + \cos\frac{\pi t}{T}\right)
$$

Smoothly decreases from $\eta_{\max}$ to $\eta_{\min}$ following a cosine curve. No milestone selection needed, smoother than step decay. The **SGDR** variant (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016) adds warm restarts — after lr reaches $\eta_{\min}$, it resets to $\eta_{\max}$ and repeats with progressively shorter cycles.

### Cosine + warmup

Combines linear warmup with cosine decay — the **current de facto standard** for most deep learning:

$$
\eta_t = \begin{cases}
\eta_{\max} \cdot \dfrac{t}{T_{\text{warm}}} & t < T_{\text{warm}} \\[8pt]
\eta_{\min} + \dfrac{1}{2}(\eta_{\max} - \eta_{\min})\!\left(1 + \cos\dfrac{\pi(t - T_{\text{warm}})}{T - T_{\text{warm}}}\right) & t \geq T_{\text{warm}}
\end{cases}
$$

Used in BERT, GPT, ViT, and most modern transformers.

### WSD — Warmup, Stable, Decay

Divides training into three explicit phases:

1. **Warmup**: lr increases linearly to $\eta_{\max}$
2. **Stable**: holds $\eta_{\max}$ for the majority of training
3. **Decay**: rapidly decreases to $\eta_{\min}$ (cosine or linear) in the final 10–20%

**Advantage over cosine**: with cosine, lr is always changing — you can't pause and extend training. WSD has a fixed stable phase — if you have more data or compute, simply extend the stable phase and then decay. Much more flexible for continued pre-training.

<Note type="result">
WSD is becoming the preferred choice for LLM pre-training. MiniCPM, Llama 3, and several recent models use WSD or similar variants for their flexible continued training capability.
</Note>

## Practical comparison

| Schedule | Needs tuning | Good for | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Step decay | Milestones, γ | Vision (CNN) | Old but effective |
| Cosine | η_min | All tasks | Simple, solid |
| Cosine + warmup | T_warmup, η_min | Transformers, LLM | Current standard |
| WSD | T_warmup, T_stable | LLM pre-training | Most flexible |

## In practice

```python
# Cosine annealing (PyTorch)
scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.CosineAnnealingLR(
    optimizer, T_max=num_steps, eta_min=1e-6
)

# Cosine + warmup (HuggingFace Transformers)
from transformers import get_cosine_schedule_with_warmup
scheduler = get_cosine_schedule_with_warmup(
    optimizer,
    num_warmup_steps=num_steps // 10,
    num_training_steps=num_steps,
)
```

- **T_warmup**: 1–5% of total steps for pre-training; 5–10% for fine-tuning
- **η_min**: typically 0–10% of η_max; many LLM recipes use η_min = 0
- **η_max**: tune independently, not dependent on the schedule
- With AdamW: lr typically 1e-4 to 3e-4; with SGD: typically 0.01–0.1
