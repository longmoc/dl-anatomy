# Likelihood, Cross-Entropy & KL Divergence

In deep learning, we often say "the model predicts the right class" or "the language model predicts the next token". Under that language, many models are doing something more probabilistic:

> Assign high probability to what actually happened.

With a softmax, all alternatives must share total probability 1. So when training pushes probability up for the correct label or token, the competing choices are pushed down through normalization. We do not always have to write explicit negative examples; they are already inside the distribution.

A classifier does not only choose a class. It produces a distribution:

$$
q_\theta(y \mid x)
$$

A language model does not only emit a token. It produces a distribution:

$$
q_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})
$$

Training usually means making the model distribution $q_\theta$ close to the true data distribution $p_{\text{data}}$. Likelihood, cross-entropy, and KL divergence are three ways to view that same mismatch.

## True distribution and model distribution

Suppose data comes from a true distribution:

$$
(x, y) \sim p_{\text{data}}(x, y)
$$

We do not know the full $p_{\text{data}}$. We only have a finite dataset:

$$
\mathcal{D} = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{n}
$$

The model creates an approximate distribution:

$$
q_\theta(y \mid x)
$$

The goal is not for the model to memorize labels, but to make $q_\theta(y \mid x)$ as close as possible to the true conditional distribution $p_{\text{data}}(y \mid x)$ within the limits of data and model class.

<Note type="intuition">
When a classifier returns `[0.02, 0.91, 0.07]`, those are not just scores. They are a probability distribution the model is claiming: "for this input, I believe class 2 has probability 91%".
</Note>

## Likelihood — how much probability does the model assign to real data?

For one sample $(x_i, y_i)$, the model assigns probability:

$$
q_\theta(y_i \mid x_i)
$$

For the whole dataset, likelihood is the product of these probabilities:

$$
\operatorname{Lik}(\theta;\mathcal{D}) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} q_\theta(y_i \mid x_i)
$$

If the model assigns high probability to the correct label of every sample, likelihood is high. If even a few real labels receive very low probability, the product drops sharply.

In practice, we use log-likelihood:

$$
\log \operatorname{Lik}(\theta;\mathcal{D}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log q_\theta(y_i \mid x_i)
$$

Log turns products into sums, improves numerical stability, and is easier to optimize.

## Maximum likelihood and negative log-likelihood

**Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE)** chooses parameters that make the observed data most probable:

$$
\theta^* = \arg\max_\theta \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log q_\theta(y_i \mid x_i)
$$

Deep learning optimizers usually minimize losses, so we flip the sign:

$$
\mathcal{J}_{\text{NLL}}(\theta) = - \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log q_\theta(y_i \mid x_i)
$$

This is **negative log-likelihood**.

When reporting loss over a batch or dataset, we usually average it:

$$
\frac{1}{n}\mathcal{J}_{\text{NLL}}(\theta)
$$

The sum and the average differ only by the constant factor $1/n$, so they have the same optimum. The average is simply easier to compare across batches or datasets of different sizes.

For one sample, if the model assigns probability $0.9$ to the correct label:

$$
-\log 0.9 \approx 0.105
$$

If it assigns only $0.01$:

$$
-\log 0.01 \approx 4.605
$$

NLL heavily penalizes confident mistakes or low probability assigned to real data.

<Note type="result">
Training with NLL means making observed data **less surprising** under the model. Real data that the model considers very unlikely creates a large loss.
</Note>

## A softmax classifier is a distribution

In classification, the model usually produces logits:

$$
s = f_\theta(x)
$$

Softmax turns logits into a distribution:

$$
q_\theta(y=k \mid x) = \frac{\exp(s_k)}{\sum_j \exp(s_j)}
$$

If the correct label is class $c$, the sample NLL is:

$$
\mathcal{L}(x, c) = -\log q_\theta(y=c \mid x)
$$

This is the familiar classification loss. Softmax + NLL is not a standalone trick; it is maximum likelihood for a categorical distribution.

## Entropy — how uncertain is the true distribution?

The entropy of a distribution $p$ is:

$$
H(p) = -\sum_y p(y)\log p(y)
$$

Entropy measures the intrinsic uncertainty of a distribution.

- If $p$ puts all mass on one class, entropy is low.
- If $p$ is close to uniform, entropy is high.

For a fair coin:

$$
H(p) = -0.5\log 0.5 - 0.5\log 0.5
$$

Entropy does not depend on the model. It is a property of the true distribution. If the task is genuinely ambiguous, high entropy is unavoidable.

<Note type="intuition">
Entropy is the minimum "surprise" if we know the true distribution. Even a perfect model may face uncertainty if the phenomenon itself is uncertain.
</Note>

## Cross-entropy — encoding true data with the model

Cross-entropy between the true distribution $p$ and model distribution $q$ is:

$$
H(p, q) = -\sum_y p(y)\log q(y)
$$

It asks: if data truly comes from $p$, but we use $q$ to assign probabilities or encode it, what is the average cost?

In supervised learning, we are usually talking about a conditional distribution for each input. Written more fully:

$$
H(p_{\text{data}}, q_\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}(x)}
\left[
-\sum_y p_{\text{data}}(y \mid x)\log q_\theta(y \mid x)
\right]
$$

In words: for each input $x$, compare the true label distribution $p_{\text{data}}(y \mid x)$ with the model distribution $q_\theta(y \mid x)$, then average over inputs.

If $p$ is a one-hot label for the correct class $c$:

$$
p(y=c)=1,\qquad p(y\neq c)=0
$$

then cross-entropy becomes:

$$
H(p, q_\theta) = -\log q_\theta(y=c \mid x)
$$

So **cross-entropy loss in classification is NLL** when labels are one-hot.

## KL divergence — how far is the model from the true distribution?

KL divergence from $p$ to $q$ is:

$$
D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q) = \sum_y p(y)\log \frac{p(y)}{q(y)}
$$

It measures the extra cost of using $q$ instead of $p$.

The important identity:

$$
H(p, q) = H(p) + D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)
$$

During training, $p$ is the fixed data distribution, so $H(p)$ does not depend on $\theta$. Therefore:

$$
\arg\min_\theta H(p, q_\theta)
=
\arg\min_\theta D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q_\theta)
$$

Minimizing cross-entropy is equivalent to minimizing KL from the data distribution to the model distribution.

<Note type="result">
This is the key bridge: cross-entropy is not only a convenient loss to implement; it pulls the model distribution toward the data distribution in the direction $D_{\text{KL}}(p_{\text{data}} \| q_\theta)$.
</Note>

### A small numerical example

Suppose the true distribution is a fair coin:

$$
p = [0.5, 0.5]
$$

The model is quite biased:

$$
q = [0.9, 0.1]
$$

The entropy of the true data is:

$$
H(p) = -0.5\log 0.5 - 0.5\log 0.5 \approx 0.693
$$

Cross-entropy when we use model $q$ is:

$$
H(p,q) = -0.5\log 0.9 - 0.5\log 0.1 \approx 1.204
$$

The extra waste is exactly KL:

$$
D_{\text{KL}}(p\|q) = H(p,q) - H(p) \approx 0.511
$$

So cross-entropy has two parts: the uncertainty already present in the data, plus the extra cost from using the wrong model distribution.

The numbers above use natural logs, so the unit is **nats**. With $\log_2$, the unit would be bits.

## KL direction matters

KL is not symmetric:

$$
D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q) \neq D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)
$$

The direction $D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)$ heavily penalizes cases where $p(y)$ is high but $q(y)$ is low. In other words, the model is penalized for missing regions where real data appears. If the data has two modes, the model is pushed to cover both; dropping one mode makes KL grow sharply. This is the **mode-covering** intuition: better to cover a little too broadly than to miss real data.

The direction $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)$ heavily penalizes cases where the model puts probability mass in regions where $p$ is low. If the region between two modes does not look like data, the model avoids putting mass there. With limited capacity, it may choose one confident mode rather than spread across both. This is the more **mode-seeking** intuition.

| Direction | Intuition |
|---|---|
| $D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)$ | the model should cover real data and avoid missing modes |
| $D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)$ | the model should avoid putting mass where real data has little support |

In supervised MLE, we usually encounter $D_{\text{KL}}(p_{\text{data}} \| q_\theta)$. In variational inference, depending on the setup, we may see the other KL direction, with different behavior.

## Classification, language modeling, and generative models

These three formulas appear everywhere.

### Classification

With one-hot labels:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} = -\log q_\theta(y=c \mid x)
$$

This is the NLL of a categorical distribution.

### Language modeling

An autoregressive sequence factorizes as:

$$
q_\theta(x_1,\dots,x_T) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} q_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})
$$

Sequence NLL:

$$
-\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log q_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})
$$

To compare sequences of different lengths, we use average token cross-entropy:

$$
\mathrm{CE}_{\text{token}}
=
-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log q_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})
$$

Perplexity is:

$$
\text{PPL} = \exp(\mathrm{CE}_{\text{token}})
$$

Lower perplexity means the model assigns better average probability to the true tokens.

### Generative models

A generative model learns a distribution $q_\theta(x)$ over data. If it optimizes likelihood directly, it does:

$$
\max_\theta \sum_i \log q_\theta(x_i)
$$

Autoregressive models do this explicitly. VAEs use a lower bound because direct likelihood is hard to compute. Diffusion models use objectives related to denoising and score matching, but they still live in the larger story: learn a distribution close to the data distribution.

## Common misunderstandings

**Cross-entropy is not only for classification.** It is a concept between two distributions; one-hot classification is just the most common case.

**Likelihood is not the probability of parameters.** Likelihood is a function of $\theta$ after data has been observed. It is not $p(\theta)$; Bayesian inference places distributions over parameters.

**KL is not a distance metric.** It is asymmetric and does not satisfy the triangle inequality. Calling it a "distance" should be understood loosely.

**Low loss does not guarantee good calibration.** A model can have high accuracy but poorly calibrated probabilities. Calibration is the separate question of whether confidence matches real frequency.

## The Big Picture

| Lens | Formula | Question |
|---|---|---|
| Likelihood | $\operatorname{Lik}(\theta;\mathcal{D})$ | Does the model assign high probability to real data? |
| NLL | $-\sum_i \log q_\theta(y_i \mid x_i)$ | How surprising is real data under the model? |
| Cross-entropy | $H(p, q)$ | How costly is it to encode true data with the model? |
| KL divergence | $D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)$ | How far is the model from the true distribution? |

When $p$ is fixed, minimizing cross-entropy is equivalent to minimizing KL. When labels are one-hot, cross-entropy becomes NLL. When the model is a softmax classifier, NLL is the familiar classification loss.

That is why the same idea appears across deep learning: **training makes the model distribution less mismatched with the data distribution**.
