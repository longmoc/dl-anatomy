# Entropy, Mutual Information & Compression

In [Likelihood, Cross-Entropy & KL Divergence](/dl-anatomy/foundations/likelihood-cross-entropy-kl-en), entropy appeared as the uncertainty of a distribution. But entropy is not only a side term in cross-entropy:

$$
H(p, q) = H(p) + D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q)
$$

It is the entry point into a broader view: **information theory**. If probability asks "what can happen, and with what probability?", information theory asks:

> How much does this observation reduce uncertainty?

In deep learning, that question appears everywhere:

- how much information does a label contain about the input?
- how much useful information does an embedding keep?
- what noise can compression remove?
- how does contrastive learning increase shared information between views?
- can a bottleneck improve generalization?

## Entropy — uncertainty before observation

For a discrete random variable $X \sim p(x)$, entropy is:

$$
H(X) = -\sum_x p(x)\log p(x)
$$

Entropy measures the average uncertainty of $X$ before observing a concrete value.

| Distribution | Entropy |
|---|---|
| certain single value | lowest |
| nearly uniform over many values | high |
| heavily skewed to a few values | medium/low |

If we use $\log_2$, the unit is **bits**. If we use $\log_e$, the unit is **nats**. Deep learning usually uses natural log, so losses such as NLL and cross-entropy are often measured in nats.

<Note type="intuition">
A rare outcome carries more information than a common one. If the weather is sunny every day, "today is sunny" says little. If snow is extremely rare, observing snow reduces uncertainty much more.
</Note>

## Self-information — surprise of one outcome

The information content of one outcome $x$ is:

$$
I(x) = -\log p(x)
$$

The rarer the outcome, the smaller $p(x)$ is, and the larger $-\log p(x)$ becomes. Entropy is the expectation of self-information:

$$
H(X) = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p}[-\log p(x)]
$$

This is why negative log-likelihood has the intuition of "surprise": if the model assigns low probability to real data, that sample creates high surprise and high loss.

## Conditional entropy — how much uncertainty remains after knowing something else?

Conditional entropy measures uncertainty in $Y$ after knowing $X$:

$$
H(Y \mid X) = -\sum_{x,y}p(x,y)\log p(y\mid x)
$$

If knowing $X$ predicts $Y$ well, $H(Y\mid X)$ is low. If knowing $X$ does not help, $H(Y\mid X)$ is close to $H(Y)$.

In supervised learning, this is a foundational question:

> How much information does input $X$ contain about label $Y$?

If $Y$ is genuinely noisy or ambiguous even after knowing $X$, then no model can achieve zero loss in a probabilistic sense. Conditional entropy is the irreducible uncertainty limit.

## Mutual information — how much information do two variables share?

Mutual information between $X$ and $Y$ is:

$$
I(X;Y) = H(Y) - H(Y\mid X)
$$

It measures how much uncertainty about $Y$ is reduced by knowing $X$.

An equivalent form:

$$
I(X;Y) = \sum_{x,y}p(x,y)\log \frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)p(y)}
$$

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent:

$$
p(x,y)=p(x)p(y)
$$

then:

$$
I(X;Y)=0
$$

If knowing $X$ helps predict $Y$, mutual information is positive.

<Note type="result">
Mutual information is a more precise way to say "two quantities are related". It does not only measure linear dependence like correlation; in definition, it measures any probabilistic dependence.
</Note>

## Compression — shorter codes from better distributions

Information theory is tightly connected to coding. If an event is likely, it should receive a short code. If an event is rare, a longer code is acceptable.

Entropy $H(X)$ is the minimum average code length for samples from the true distribution $p(x)$.

Cross-entropy:

$$
H(p,q) = -\mathbb{E}_{x\sim p}[\log q(x)]
$$

is the average code length if data truly comes from $p$ but we encode it using model $q$. The extra waste is exactly KL:

$$
H(p,q) - H(p) = D_{\text{KL}}(p\|q)
$$

Under the coding view:

- entropy: minimum cost if we know the true distribution
- cross-entropy: cost when using the model distribution
- KL: wasted nats/bits because the model is wrong

## Compression in representation learning

Representation learning often wants to keep useful information and discard irrelevant detail. If input is $X$, representation is $Z$, and target is $Y$, a common intuition is:

- $Z$ should keep information about $Y$
- $Z$ should not keep every detail of $X$

<InformationBottleneckDiagram lang="en" />

This is the spirit of the **information bottleneck**:

$$
\max I(Z;Y) - \beta I(Z;X)
$$

where:

- $I(Z;Y)$ encourages the representation to keep target-predictive information
- $I(Z;X)$ is controlled so the representation does not copy the entire input
- $\beta$ controls the compression level

This formula should not be treated as a simple recipe for training every model. Mutual information is often hard to estimate accurately in deep learning, especially in continuous and high-dimensional spaces. But it is a powerful lens: a good representation does not keep everything; it keeps the right parts.

<Note type="warning">
"Compression" does not mean making vectors small at all costs. A small representation that loses important signal is bad. Useful compression removes nuisance variation, not the signal itself.
</Note>

## Mutual information and contrastive learning

Contrastive learning is often described as pulling positive pairs together and pushing negatives apart. From the information-theoretic view, it can also be read as learning representations that preserve shared information between two views.

Suppose we have two augmentations of the same sample:

$$
v_1 = a_1(x), \qquad v_2 = a_2(x)
$$

The encoder produces:

$$
z_1=f_\theta(v_1), \qquad z_2=f_\theta(v_2)
$$

The contrastive objective encourages $z_1$ and $z_2$ to share information that remains stable under augmentation. If augmentation is well chosen, that stable part is often semantic content.

InfoNCE is connected to a lower bound on mutual information under certain conditions. But the important intuition is:

> A positive pair defines "what information should be kept"; augmentation defines "what information may be discarded".

If augmentation is wrong, the model can learn the wrong invariance. In medical imaging, too much color distortion may remove diagnostic signal; in OCR, aggressive cropping can remove the character itself.

## Entropy in classification and calibration

A classifier output is a distribution:

$$
q_\theta(y\mid x)
$$

The entropy of the output:

$$
H(q_\theta(\cdot\mid x))
$$

measures the uncertainty the model expresses for that input.

| Output | Entropy | Intuition |
|---|---|---|
| `[0.99, 0.01]` | low | the model is very confident |
| `[0.55, 0.45]` | higher | the model is unsure |
| uniform over many classes | high | the model nearly does not know |

But low entropy does not mean the model is correct. A model can be confidently wrong. Calibration asks a different question:

> Among predictions made with 90% confidence, is the model correct about 90% of the time?

Entropy says whether the model is uncertain; calibration says whether that uncertainty is trustworthy.

## Entropy regularization

Sometimes entropy is added to the objective to control the sharpness of a policy or distribution.

In reinforcement learning, an entropy bonus encourages exploration:

$$
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{reward}} - \alpha H(\pi(\cdot\mid s))
$$

High entropy means the policy has not put all probability on one action, so it still explores.

In classification, label smoothing can also be read as a way to avoid an overly sharp target distribution. Instead of a strict one-hot target, a small amount of probability mass is assigned to other classes. This reduces overconfidence and can improve calibration.

## Minimum description length and generalization

Compression also connects to generalization through an old intuition: a model that explains data with a shorter description is often more trustworthy than a model that must memorize too many random details.

**Minimum Description Length (MDL)** reads learning as a coding problem:

$$
\text{total length} = \text{model description} + \text{data description given model}
$$

An overly simple model has a short description but does not compress data well. An overly complex model may compress train data well but has a long model description or learns noise. The good region trades off the two.

In modern deep learning, this connection does not become one simple formula for all large models. But the compression lens remains useful for reading:

- regularization
- flat minima
- pruning/quantization
- representation bottlenecks
- self-supervised pretraining

## Common misunderstandings

**High entropy is not always bad.** If a task is genuinely ambiguous, high entropy is honest. Forcing confidence on ambiguous data can make calibration worse.

**High mutual information is not always good.** A representation that keeps too much input information may also keep noise, background, shortcuts, or private information.

**Compression is not the same as dimensionality reduction.** A vector with the same dimension can still carry less information if noise, dropout, quantization, or a bottleneck objective controls it.

**The information-theoretic lens does not replace evaluation.** MI and entropy give strong intuition, but estimators in high-dimensional neural networks can be difficult and biased.

## The Big Picture

| Concept | Question |
|---|---|
| Self-information | How surprising is this outcome? |
| Entropy | Before observing, how uncertain are we? |
| Conditional entropy | After knowing $X$, how much uncertainty remains about $Y$? |
| Mutual information | How much does knowing $X$ reduce uncertainty about $Y$? |
| Compression | How can data or representations be encoded more compactly? |
| Information bottleneck | What should a representation keep about the target and discard from the input? |

From this view, learning is not only loss reduction. It is also the organization of information: keep signal, discard noise, compress unnecessary detail, and create representations rich enough to predict without copying the entire input.
