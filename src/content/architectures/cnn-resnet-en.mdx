# CNN & ResNet

## Convolution — an inductive bias for images

A fully-connected layer wires every pixel to every neuron — for a $224\times224$ image that's billions of parameters, and it ignores an obvious fact: **nearby pixels are related**. Convolution bakes in exactly that assumption through two principles:

- **Locality**: each neuron looks at only a small region (the receptive field) — e.g. a $3\times3$ kernel
- **Weight sharing**: the same kernel slides across the whole image → it detects one feature (edge, corner) at *every* position

$$
(I * K)(x, y) = \sum_{i}\sum_{j} I(x+i,\, y+j)\, K(i, j)
$$

These two principles give **translation invariance** (a shifted object is still recognized) and a huge parameter reduction. Stacking conv layers grows the receptive field — features progress from edges → textures → parts → objects.

<Note type="intuition">
Weight sharing is a strong form of regularization: instead of learning per-position, the model is forced to learn features that are *useful everywhere*. This is why CNNs are far more data-efficient than MLPs on images.
</Note>

**AlexNet (2012)** was the moment deep learning truly took off: a deep CNN dominated ImageNet, igniting a decade of vision research.

## The depth problem

Intuition says: deeper = more powerful. But in practice, stacking too many layers makes **training error go up** — not overfitting (both train and test get worse), but **degradation**: deep nets are harder to optimize.

The core cause is the **vanishing gradient**: as the gradient flows backward through each layer it's multiplied by some factor; if that factor is $< 1$, the gradient decays exponentially toward the early layers — which then barely receive any learning signal.

## ResNet — a highway for gradients

**He et al. (2015)** solved it with a small but profound change: instead of learning the mapping $H(x)$ directly, a layer learns the **residual** $F(x) = H(x) - x$, then adds back the input:

$$
y = F(x) + x
$$

The derivative during backprop:

$$
\frac{\partial y}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} + 1
$$

The $+1$ term is the key: however small $\frac{\partial F}{\partial x}$ becomes, the gradient still has a "highway" straight through the identity, undiminished. This let ResNet train networks of **over 1000 layers**.

<Note type="result">
Skip connections also make it **easy to learn the identity**: if a layer isn't needed, it just drives $F(x) \to 0$ and you immediately get $y = x$. A deep net is never worse than its shallower counterpart — degradation disappears.
</Note>

## After ResNet

Residual connections became a standard component of nearly every deep architecture since — including the Transformer. A few notable CNN branches:

- **Efficient CNNs** (MobileNet, EfficientNet): depthwise-separable convolutions, fewer FLOPs for mobile devices
- **Modern CNNs** (ConvNeXt): borrow design ideas from Transformers, showing CNNs remain competitive on vision
