# Loss Landscape & Non-convexity

## Background: convexity in classical optimization

In classical ML (linear regression, logistic regression, SVM), the first step is often to verify whether the loss function is convex:

- **Convex function**: every local minimum is a global minimum → gradient descent is guaranteed to converge to the best solution
- **Non-convex function**: may get stuck at a local minimum → no guarantees

The convexity check: the Hessian $H = \nabla^2 \mathcal{L}(\theta)$ must be **positive semi-definite** everywhere — all eigenvalues $\geq 0$.

For multi-layer neural networks, the loss is almost certainly **not convex**. Yet in practice, nobody checks convexity before training GPT-4. Why?

## High dimensionality → saddle points, not local minima

A critical point ($\nabla\mathcal{L} = 0$) is a **local minimum** only if all $d$ eigenvalues of the Hessian are positive — i.e. the landscape curves upward in every direction. A **single** negative eigenvalue makes it a saddle point: curving up in some directions, down in others.

If we treat each eigenvalue as independently positive with probability $\frac{1}{2}$:

$$
P(\text{local minimum}) = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^d
$$

| Parameters $d$ | $P(\text{local minimum})$ |
|---|---|
| 10 | $\approx 0.1\%$ |
| 100 | $\approx 10^{-30}$ |
| 1000 | $\approx 10^{-300}$ |
| $10^9$ (small LLM) | $\approx 10^{-300{,}000{,}000}$ |

For networks with millions to billions of parameters, the probability that a random critical point is a local minimum is essentially zero. **Almost all critical points in large networks are saddle points** — the core difference from low-dimensional optimization, where bad local minima are the main concern.

### Random Matrix Theory — Wigner Semicircle Law

Why is the "each eigenvalue positive with probability $\frac{1}{2}$" approximation reasonable? Random Matrix Theory tells us that the eigenvalue distribution of a random symmetric matrix (like the Hessian of a randomly initialized network) follows the **Wigner Semicircle Law**:

$$
\rho(\lambda) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2}\sqrt{4\sigma^2 - \lambda^2}
$$

This distribution is **symmetric around 0** — roughly half the eigenvalues are negative, half positive. A random critical point almost always has both upward- and downward-curving directions → it's a saddle point.

**Dauphin et al. (2014)** refined this picture: in real networks, the **index** of a critical point (fraction of negative eigenvalues) strongly correlates with the loss at that point. High-loss critical point → many negative eigenvalues (a "steep" saddle). Low-loss critical point → few negative eigenvalues, close to a true local minimum. In other words: the lower the loss, the more the point resembles a good local minimum — and bad saddle points mostly sit in high-loss regions.

### Saddle point vs plateau

Both produce small gradients, but the mechanism and the fix differ:

| | Saddle point | Plateau |
|---|---|---|
| Gradient $\nabla\mathcal{L}$ | $= 0$ | $\approx 0$ |
| Hessian | Has negative eigenvalues (escape directions) | All eigenvalues $\approx 0$ |
| Nature | Saddle — there's a way down | Flat region — no clear direction |
| Fix | SGD noise (projects onto negative directions) | Momentum, Adam (accumulate steps) |

### How gradient descent escapes saddle points

- **Exact GD (full-batch)**: at a saddle point $\nabla\mathcal{L} = 0$ exactly → zero update → **stuck** indefinitely in theory.
- **SGD**: the noise $\epsilon_t$ in the gradient projects onto the Hessian's negative-eigenvalue directions → pushes θ off the saddle along the downward-curving direction → **escapes naturally**, with no intervention.
- **Perturbed GD (Jin et al., 2017)**: adds deliberate noise when the gradient is small → provably escapes saddle points in polynomial time, with rigorous theoretical guarantees.

<Note type="result">
This is a key reason SGD outperforms full-batch GD on non-convex losses: noise isn't a flaw to be removed, but a mechanism for **automatically escaping saddle points** — something full-batch GD lacks entirely.
</Note>

## All good local minima are equivalent

### Permutation symmetry

Consider a simple network with 2 interchangeable neurons:

```
Input → [neuron A, neuron B] → Output
Swap weights of A and B:
  → Same function
  → Same loss value
  → Different point in parameter space
```

A network with $L$ layers and $n$ neurons per layer has $n!^L$ such points, all with **identical loss**. This is the simplest example of a deeper phenomenon: parameter space contains countless "equally good" points due to the network's permutation symmetry.

### Choromanska et al. (2015) — spin-glass model

*"The Loss Surfaces of Multilayer Networks"* was one of the first papers to systematically characterize this. The key idea: under some simplifying assumptions, the neural network loss surface maps onto a **spin-glass model** from statistical physics — a well-studied physical system.

The result: for $n \to \infty$:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta^*_{\text{local}}) \approx \mathcal{L}(\theta^*_{\text{global}})
$$

Local minima distribute in a pattern: bad local minima cluster in the low-complexity (underfitting) regime; good local minima cluster in the overparameterized regime.

<LocalMinimaViz type="spinglass" lang="en" />

<Note type="warning">
The paper makes strong simplifying assumptions that don't fully match modern transformers. But its intuition and empirical observations have been widely confirmed since.
</Note>

### Mode Connectivity — Garipov et al. (2018)

Good local minima don't just have equivalent loss — they're **connected to each other** by paths along which the loss stays low.

**Experiment**: train the same network twice with different random seeds → $\theta_A$ and $\theta_B$. Two ways to connect them:

**Linear interpolation** (straight line):

$$
\theta(t) = (1-t)\theta_A + t\theta_B, \quad t \in [0,1]
$$

→ Loss **spikes** in the middle — the two minima are separated by a high barrier.

**Bézier curve** through an optimized midpoint $\theta_{\text{mid}}$:

$$
\theta(t) = \text{BezierCurve}(\theta_A,\; \theta_B,\; \theta_{\text{mid}})
$$

→ Loss **barely changes** throughout the journey from $\theta_A$ to $\theta_B$.

<LocalMinimaViz type="mode" lang="en" />

**Draxler et al. (2018)** generalized further: in large enough networks, minima connect via **straight lines** (no curved path needed). **Benton et al. (2021)** extended this to *simplexes* — not just curves but entire planes and subspaces connecting minima at low loss.

<Note type="theorem">
Good local minima aren't isolated holes — they lie on the same **connected flat region** in parameter space. "Many minima" is really one large region that gradient descent, once it enters, stays stable in.
</Note>

### Flat vs Sharp — not all good minima are equal

Everything above argues that good local minima are "equivalent" — but there is one important distinction: not all good local minima **generalize** equally well. Measured by the Hessian:

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta + \epsilon) - \mathcal{L}(\theta) \approx \frac{1}{2}\epsilon^T H \epsilon
$$

<LocalMinimaViz type="flatsharp" lang="en" />

| | Sharp minimum | Flat minimum |
|---|---|---|
| Eigenvalues of $H$ | Large | Small |
| Small perturbation $\epsilon$ | Large loss increase | Stable loss |
| Distribution shift (train→test) | Test loss spikes → overfits | Test loss barely changes → generalizes well |
| Found by SGD? | Rarely — noise "shakes it out" | Frequently — occupies larger volume |

#### Why SGD is biased toward flat minima

The probability of stopping at a minimum is proportional to the **volume** of the surrounding region:

$$
p(\text{stopping at minimum}) \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{\det H}}
$$

A flat minimum has small $\det H$ → higher stopping probability — not because SGD "knows" flat is better, but simply because flat minima **occupy more volume** in parameter space.

**Keskar et al. (2017)** confirmed this empirically: small batch → more noise → SGD finds flat minima → better generalization. Large batch → less noise → sharp minima → worse generalization.

**Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997)** — the classic paper formally defining flat minima and connecting them to MDL (Minimum Description Length): a flat minimum corresponds to a model with **lower description complexity**.

## SGD noise as a natural regularizer

In classical ML, a regularizer is added **explicitly** to the loss:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}}(\theta) = \mathcal{L}(\theta) + \lambda\|\theta\|^2
$$

SGD does the same thing on its own — but **implicitly**, with no extra term.

### The structure of SGD noise

The gradient SGD actually uses is a stochastic estimate of the true gradient:

$$
\tilde{g}_t = \nabla\mathcal{L}(\theta_t) + \epsilon_t
$$

$$
\text{Cov}(\epsilon_t) = \frac{1}{|B|}\Sigma(\theta_t)
$$

where $\Sigma(\theta_t) = \text{Cov}_{i \sim \text{data}}[\nabla\ell_i(\theta_t)]$ is the **gradient covariance matrix** — a property of the data distribution at the current position $\theta_t$.

Two important properties:

**1. Noise is inversely proportional to batch size** — $\text{Var}(\epsilon_t) \propto \frac{1}{|B|}$. Smaller batch → more noise → stronger regularization.

**2. Noise depends on position $\theta$** — in low-loss regions, individual gradients $\nabla\ell_i$ tend to agree → $\Sigma$ small → little noise → stable. At a sharp minimum, the $\nabla\ell_i$ scatter → $\Sigma$ large → more noise → the model gets pushed away. This is a self-stabilizing mechanism.

### SGD ≈ Langevin Dynamics

When the learning rate is small and steps are treated as continuous, SGD approximates a **Stochastic Differential Equation (Langevin dynamics)**:

$$
d\theta = -\nabla\mathcal{L}(\theta)\,dt + \sqrt{\eta \cdot \Sigma(\theta)}\,dW_t
$$

where $dW_t$ is a Wiener process (Brownian motion). The system's effective temperature:

$$
T_{\text{eff}} \propto \frac{\eta}{|B|}
$$

The **stationary distribution** of this Langevin system:

$$
p(\theta) \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{\mathcal{L}(\theta)}{T_{\text{eff}}}\right)
$$

This is exactly the **Gibbs distribution** from statistical physics — and the **Bayesian posterior** under a uniform prior on $\theta$.

<Note type="theorem">
SGD doesn't just find a point $\theta^*$ — theoretically, it **samples from the posterior** over $\theta$ at "temperature" $T_{\text{eff}} = \eta/|B|$. Large learning rate, small batch → high temperature → SGD explores the landscape broadly. Small learning rate, large batch → low temperature → SGD sticks close to the current region.
</Note>

### Implicit regularizer

The expected loss after one SGD step with noise $\epsilon$:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\mathcal{L}(\theta_{t+1})] \approx \mathcal{L}(\theta_t) - \eta\|\nabla\mathcal{L}\|^2 + \underbrace{\frac{\eta^2}{2}\,\text{tr}(H\Sigma)}_{\text{noise penalty}}
$$

The term $\frac{\eta^2}{2}\,\text{tr}(H\Sigma)$ is an **implicit regularizer**: large when $H$ is large (sharp region) → SGD is penalized for staying in high-curvature areas; small when $H$ is small (flat region) → SGD is comfortable there. No need to add $\lambda\|\theta\|^2$ — SGD automatically penalizes sharp regions.

### Linear scaling rule

From $T_{\text{eff}} \propto \frac{\eta}{|B|}$:

> Increasing batch size by $k$ ≡ decreasing temperature by $k$ → learning rate must increase by $k$ to compensate. This is why, when training across many GPUs (larger batch), practitioners scale LR by the number of GPUs — not folklore, but grounded in the SDE theory.

| Batch size | Converges to | Generalization |
|---|---|---|
| Small | Flat minima | Good |
| Large | Sharp minima | Worse |

## Overparameterization creates a solution space

When $|\theta| \gg n_{\text{data}}$, the system:

$$
f_\theta(x_i) = y_i, \quad i = 1, \ldots, n
$$

is **underdetermined** — far fewer equations than unknowns. The solution set is not an isolated point but a **manifold**:

$$
\dim(\mathcal{M}^*) \approx |\theta| - n
$$

| Model | $|\theta|$ | $n_{\text{data}}$ (approx) | $\dim(\mathcal{M}^*)$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| ResNet-50 | ~25M | ~1.2M (ImageNet) | ~24M |
| GPT-3 | ~175B | ~300B tokens* | very large |
| LLaMA-3 70B | ~70B | >1T tokens | very large |

*token count ≠ independent constraints directly*

The set of $\theta$ achieving low loss is a **wide region**, not an isolated point. When gradient descent enters this region, it "gets stuck" in a good sense — loss doesn't increase even as parameters move around within it.

### Neural Tangent Kernel (Jacot et al., 2018)

In the infinite-width limit, $K = JJ^T$ is fixed throughout training:

$$
\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{dt} = -K \cdot \mathcal{L} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{exponential convergence, linear dynamics}
$$

At infinite width, neural networks are **convex in function space** (though not in parameter space). As networks grow larger, the function-space landscape becomes progressively easier to optimize.

## The full picture

| Observation | Mechanism | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Many local minima have equivalent low loss | Symmetry + overparameterization | No need to find the global minimum |
| Good minima are connected | Loss landscape is a connected manifold | Gradient descent doesn't get trapped in good regions |
| Flat minima generalize better | Small Hessian eigenvalues → stable under perturbation | SGD + small batch naturally prefers generalization |
| Bad local minima occur at high loss | Probability decreases with model size (Choromanska) | Gradient descent rarely encounters them |

## What we still don't understand

- **Grokking** (Power et al., 2022): models suddenly "understand" a task after many overfit epochs — why does the loss landscape shift so dramatically so late in training?
- **Double descent** (Belkin et al., 2019): test error can rise and then fall again as model complexity increases — breaks the classical bias-variance tradeoff.
- **LLM loss landscapes in practice**: both the spin-glass theory and NTK make assumptions that don't fully match real transformers. The true landscape remains a black box.

<Note type="paper">
The honest answer: deep learning **works well in practice** because of these phenomena, but theory is running 5–10 years behind empirical practice. This is why researchers keep asking "why does it work?" rather than "does it work?"
</Note>
